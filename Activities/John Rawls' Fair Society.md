# John Rawls' Fair Society

Activity inspired by John Rawls' concept of veil of ignorance.

### Goal

Explore what fairness really means, learn to take the perspective of others
Understand the complexity behind ethical and political positions, encourage true open-mindedness - not just "tolerating" but engaging with disagreement.

> [!hint]  
> Banger pour PDPEF observations

### Description

This is a deep-thinking activity. Some kids might find it slow or abstract, but others will engage with it profoundly. Run it **later in the camp**, once the group is used to open discussions.

It consists of two sessions:

- **Session 1** : Create a "Fair Society"
- **Session 2** : Reality check — is it still fair when you're not in the position you imagined?

> [!attention] DO NOT REVEAL TO THE KIDS THAT IT IS A TWO PARTS ACTIVITY !! 

Don't wait too many days between the sessions. All leaders should be well-prepared and understand the philosophy behind the activity.

##### Session 1 : Building a fair society

- Create rules for a fair society
- These rules cannot be changed later unless explicitly stated with conditions.
- Encourage specific, applicable rules (e.g., "If someone hurts someone else, what happens?").

##### Session 2 : Flipping The Script

Leaders assign each participant a role that challenges their own constitution. The question is: **Are the rules still fair now that you’re on the other side?**

The leader takes (mental) notes of which kids takes what position on what topic.

> [!example]
> 
> - If a kid supports the death penalty, assign them the role of someone wrongfully convicted, or someone who stole medicine to save a loved one.
> - If a kid is against the death penalty, assign them the role of someone whose friend was permanently disabled because he was attacked by someone who repeatedly commits crimes.
> - If they believe in equal income, assign them a role of someone highly altruistic and hardworking (e.g a doctor) — but whose neighbor is lazy and destructive.
> - If they are for a healthcare, tell them they take their health seriously but have to pay taxes, of course, to finance it, while a lot of people smoke, don't exercise, eat fast food... And will require health assistance later in life. Is it fair for you to pay for their mistakes ? 
> - You are 22 and diagnosed with cancer, treatment is 200k and you are bankrupt
> - You wanted tougher punishments, now your brother is in prison for stealing an apple
> - You wanted only rehab, now your sister was attacked by someone who was sent to therapy
> - You support free speech, someone says something racist, or accuses you of being XYZ
> - You say no cars, no plastic, no meat -> you live in a rural area
> - You don't care about ecology -> in 30 years, your cousin is diagnosed with lung cancer from pollution, in the summer you can't go outside and you are hungry because crops don't grow
> - ...

### Instructions

Involve as many adults as possible. Staff should try to participate.

JCs benefit best from this activity by being their own parrallel group.

Divide the kids in groups, with at least one Leader in each group.
Give each group something to write on (give the Leader some extra paper).
Their instructions are to create rules for a perfect society.
They can go find a nice spot on campsite.
The Supervising Leader planning this activity should try to be going around and see if everything is working fine.
It's fine for some kids to zone out during this activity, try to involve them, but if they repeatedly show disinterest, don't force them. Focus on the ones that are involved.

There is no right or wrong in this activity.
It's only about questioning your reasoning, and realizing how people who disagree with you view things differently, and are maybe not plainly wrong.
This activity is cognitively challenging to leaders as well, and can even be frustrating. Try as much as you can to think of why people support a point of view, even if you don't agree with it, to think of counterarguements.

> [!hint] 
> There is no "right" or "wrong" in **beliefs**, but there is incoherence. If the goal is to encourage understanding and maybe tolerating other beliefs, some are simply incoherent and thus can be "attacked". 

- Try to be pragmatic : "Nobody can hurt anybody" is not a rule. WHAT happens if someone hurts someone ?
- As a leader, try your best to not let your personal convictions influence the kids
- Take notes of what the kids say, it's very revealing for PDPEF
- Your role is to think of the cases where these rules fail (cf examples above)

You don't have to cover all topics, even if they stick to only one, it's good as long as they are involved.

But if the kids run out of ideas, you can ask about :

> [!attention] Adapt the questions to the to age group !

###### Justice and Crime

- how to deal with criminality ?
	- rehab or punishment ? *are they here to protect the society or to change behavior ?*
	- death penalty ?
	- should kids be punished too ?
	- who should decide of punishment ? the justice or the victims ?

###### Economy

- meritocracy :
	- should people who do more, get more ?
	- should smarter people get more ?
- inheritance : should people be able to pass wealth to their children ?
- should there be a wealth cap ?
- is there a global basic income ?
	- who pays for it ?
	- is there any requirement to be eligible (to have it) ?

###### Health

- is there a healthcare ? how does it work ?
- should everyone be an organ donor by default ?
- mandatory vaccination ?

###### Education

- private schools : should you be able to pay for better education ?
- is university free ? who pays for it ?

###### Climate change

- Can you limit someone’s lifestyle for the planet? *less meat, only flying 3-4 times in a lifetime, not allowing fast fashion...*

###### Responsibilities

- Should parity exist ? Where ?
- Can everybody vote ?
	- the well informed only ?
	- not the elders ?
	- from what age ?
	- IQ tests ?
- Is there a mandatory military service ? community service ?
- Surveillance : should the government watch people 


### Suggested Debrief

Debrief doesn't need to be in the big group, they can stick to their little groups.
Also because groups tend to finsih not at the same time, and because it allows for a more "personalized" debrief. And some kids might want to talk a lot, which is too competitive in a big group. 

The debrief can be very leader dependant, which means the adult responsible for his group decides if they need a real debrief, what they are gonna talk about...

- [ ] *Open questions first*
	- [ ] Rating (thumbs, first to five, temperature...)
	- [ ] What did you think of this activity ?
	- [ ] What did you like and dislike ?
- [ ] Were there rules you thought were fair, until you had to live under them ?
- [ ] Did you change your mind on something ?
