# Updated Red Card Blue Card

### Goal

Understand cooperation more deeply.
Cooperation increases "[group fitness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitness_(biology))", but can get you exploited and is not always good. You only benefit from it if your partner is cooperating at most times too.

### Description

> [!todo] créer vidéo youtube

> [!tip] 
> You can make a first activity earlier in camp, the classic "blue card red card", where it's strategically better to defect, to show them in what conditions cooperation is not working.

This activity illustrates the Iterated Prisoner's Dilemna, where you can cooperate with a partner or "cheat" (defect), resulting in different results or gains ("payoffs").
The kids have interactions between each others, where they can choose wether to cooperate or not, by voting either blue or red.
If everyone cooperates, everybody gets points, if one "cheats", the ones who offered their trust are robbed points by the "cheaters".

More suited for older participants, but proven to work for as low as village too.

> [!attention] 
> This activity must be well understood by the organizer, which requires basic game theory knowledge (understanding the prisoner's dilemna, the iterated prisoner's dilemna, and pareto dominating strategies).

Different variations of this game exist.

> Be careful to not make a game where the dominant strategy would be to defect only.

For cooperation to make sense, at least one of these requirements must exist : 

1. Partner choice (aka punishing the people who exploit you, by excluding them)
2. Evaluating group fitness instead of personal fitness

If done wrong, the dominant strategy in this game is to always pick red.
But if done right, cheaters can :

1. be punished by being excluded of transactions (no one trusts them, no one wants to do transactions / games with them) (which is the criteria of partner choice).
2. perform less good as a group compared to a group of cooperators (which is the "group fitness evaluation" criteria)

> [!warning] 
> Trust can be fragilised by this activity if the kids just met. Different people have different visions of games, some will take them very seriously, for others it's just a game. We do not recommend kickstarting cooperation games with this activity, rather using it towards the end of camp when the kids are bonded and differentiate between "game attitude" and real personality.

> [!tip] 
> - If the kids didn't reach the stage of excluding free riders, you can do the activity a second time another time, to see if they improved.
> - You can have some JCs play and have fixed strategies (only cooperation or only defection).

### Instructions

Give the kids ~10 tiny little sheets of paper.
They can mingle and find someone to play with. They go and find a Leader or JC to supervise their interaction.
They then play, by showing at the same time either a red or blue card, to signify cooperation or defection (or a hand gesture if you don't have any materials).
If they both cooperate, they win 1 tiny paper each (given by the supervising Leader). If they both defect, they lose one each. If one cooperates and the other doesn't, the defecter takes 2 off the cooperator.

> - [ ] Can you think of situations where there is a prisoner's dilemna ? *(investing in a powerful military, getting a good lawyer for a divorce*
> - [ ] What did this activity make you think of ?
