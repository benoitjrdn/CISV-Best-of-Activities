# Overton's window and Framing

> [!wip] 
> Un peu WIP mais je verrai en planifiant

### Goal

Understand Overton's window : how our perception is based on comparison, and why extremes always seem extreme.

### Description

Two groups, getting asked how outrageous some propositions are.
They have to give a rating for each proposition.

Group 1 gets a narrow spectrum of propositions, ranging from "not at all outrageous" to "mildly outrageous."

Group 2 gets the full spectrum, from "not at all" to "extremely outrageous" — including the same propositions as Group 1.





The mildly outrageous should have different ratings across the groups.

Gather the groups and explain how Overton's window works.

### Instructions

#### Planning

Prepare 3 kinds of propositions :

- Very acceptable (group 1 & 2 in common)
- Okayish / Debatable (group 1 & 2 in common)
- Somehow extreme propositions (group 2 only)
- Totally propositions (group 2 only)

Will work better if all the propositions are on a singular topic.

#### Doing

Split into two groups.

Group 1 and 2 don't need to be groups of discussion. The kids can just be assigned a number and go to a different spot, while writing down the rating for each proposition.

If you can, put one girl and one boy of each delegation, to minimize potential cultural bias.

Read the propositions to the group.
The groups should go over each proposition without debating about it too much. It's more about emotional response to those questions than very thoughtful decision-making.
Give them about 10-20 seconds for each question, less for obvious ones.

At the end of the activity, calculate the mean of the "debatable" questions both groups had in common (you can also calculate the spread if you want).

Debrief.
